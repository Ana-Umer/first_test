First Italo-Ethiopian War
The First Italo-Ethiopian War (1895–1896) was a conflict between the Kingdom of Italy and the Ethiopian Empire over control and sovereignty in the Horn of Africa. It ended with a decisive Ethiopian victory that preserved the country's independence and became a landmark event in African resistance to European colonization.

Key facts
Dates: 1895–1896

Main belligerents: Kingdom of Italy vs. Ethiopian Empire

Major battle: Battle of Adwa (March 1, 1896)

Ethiopian leaders: Menelik II, Empress Taytu Betul

Outcome: Ethiopian victory; Italy recognized Ethiopia’s independence

Background
Following Italy’s late entry into the Scramble for Africa, it sought to expand from its colony in Eritrea into the Ethiopian highlands. The Treaty of Wuchale, signed after Menelik II’s coronation, became contentious when the Italian and Amharic versions diverged—Italy claimed a protectorate over Ethiopia, while the Ethiopian text did not. Menelik’s rejection of this claim precipitated war.

Course of the War
Italian forces advanced from Eritrea in 1895, but Ethiopia mobilized a large, multi-ethnic army armed with modern weapons obtained through trade. After several skirmishes, both sides clashed at Adwa in northern Ethiopia. Superior Ethiopian numbers, leadership, and knowledge of terrain overwhelmed Italian troops, leading to one of the most significant defeats of a colonial power in Africa.

Aftermath and Significance
The resulting Treaty of Addis Ababa nullified Italy’s protectorate claim and formally acknowledged Ethiopian sovereignty. The victory at Adwa enhanced Menelik II’s prestige, inspired anti-colonial movements across Africa and the African diaspora, and established Ethiopia as a symbol of resistance to European imperialism. Italy’s defeat tempered its colonial ambitions until renewed aggression decades later in the Second Italo‑Ethiopian War.
